# Kalinauskas Steering Wheel

## Overview

The **Kalinauskas steering wheel** (`штурвал Калинаускаса`) is a Socionics interpretive schema associated with Igor Kalinauskas and later Reinin-linked teaching materials. It is used to visualize a type of information metabolism, its motivating and problematic zones, and some intertype-relation dynamics.

In PsyCalc, the steering wheel should be treated as a **school-specific / authorial Socionics model**, not as the canonical Model A and not as a replacement for Model A.

## Attribution and Source Base

The accessible online source base has three layers:

1. A third-party mirror of Igor Kalinauskas's *Соционика. Умение общаться эффективно*, with a section explicitly titled “Штурвал Калинаускаса.”
2. The dedicated site `shturval.me`, which says it focuses on the steering-wheel model rather than Model A and cites Grigory Reinin's lectures/book *Соционика: Типология. Малые группы.* as its main source.
3. Later practitioner articles and explanations that show continued use but should not be treated as primary evidence.

See [[kalinauskas-steering-wheel-source-notes]] for source details.

## Relation to Model A

The steering wheel is **Model-A-adjacent** because it works with Socionics TIMs, functions/aspects, and intertype relations. However, it is not the same structure as [[socionics-model-a]].

Model A usually presents eight function positions such as leading, creative, role, vulnerable, suggestive, mobilizing, ignoring, and demonstrative. The steering wheel instead uses a different practical vocabulary:

- I / -I;
- II / -II;
- III / -III;
- IV / -IV;
- “good,” “bad,” “must,” “should not,” “problems,” “solutions,” “want,” and “fear.”

The dedicated steering-wheel site explicitly contrasts its focus with Model A, so PsyCalc should keep these pages separate.

## Core Schema

The steering wheel presents eight labeled zones:

| Label | Russian | English Gloss | Source Meaning |
|-------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| I | Хорошо | Good | principle of existence; level of personality organization |
| -I | Плохо | Bad | ignoring zone; minus-values |
| II | Надо | Must / Need | creativity; level of functioning |
| -II | Не надо | Should not / Not needed | norms and behavioral stereotypes |
| III | Проблемы | Problems | self-assertion and self-evaluation; least-resistance place |
| -III | Решения | Solutions | self-realization; protection of self-esteem |
| IV | Хочу | Want | motives; suggestive zone; evaluation of place |
| -IV | Боюсь | Fear | fear zone; suspiciousness and avoidance themes |

These labels are more existential and motivational than the standard Model A position names. They are useful for understanding the author's practical language, but they should not be silently substituted for Model A terms.

## Practical Reading of the Zones

### I — “Good”

The first zone is described as the principle of existence: the place where the person feels they “are,” knows themselves, and defends core self-organization. In the source language, attacks here may be experienced as attacks on the person's basic principle of existence.

### -I — “Bad”

The negative first zone is described as the ignored or minus-value zone: a part of reality the person may not notice, may reject, or may experience as “bad” from inside the steering-wheel schema.

### II — “Must / Need”

The second zone is described as functioning, creativity, and risk. It is the area through which the person demonstrates what they can do and how they operate.

### -II — “Should Not”

The negative second zone is described as norms, stereotypes, and conservative behavioral templates: what is treated as unnecessary or not-to-be-done.

### III — “Problems”

The third zone is described as a self-evaluation and problem zone. It is associated with psychological sensitivity, need for positive reinforcement, and defensive strategies around self-esteem.

This resembles, but should not be collapsed into, the Model A vulnerable/role vocabulary without source-specific care.

### -III — “Solutions”

The negative third zone is described as the way problems are solved or self-esteem is protected. In the Kalinauskas excerpt, problem/solution dynamics are especially important when comparing a type with its opposite type.

### IV — “Want”

The fourth zone is described as suggestive, motivational, and open to influence. It is where a person may seek orientation from others and evaluate whether a place or situation feels desirable.

### -IV — “Fear”

The negative fourth zone is described as fears, suspiciousness, and avoided themes. It is also discussed in relation to professional fit and psychological cost.

## Relation to Intertype Relations

The steering wheel is used to visualize standard Socionics relation classes, including identity, duality, mirror, activation, semi-duality, kindred, mirage, business, super-ego, conflict, quasi-identity, supervision, and benefit/order relations.

One distinctive feature in the Kalinauskas excerpt is the strong emphasis on **opposite types**. In the ILE / ILI example, opposite types are described as informationally complementary in the sense that they reveal ignored or denied parts of the type's informational world.

This is not the same as the mainstream Socionics habit of treating duality as the canonical complementary relation. Therefore, PsyCalc should mark “opposite type as true complement” as an authorial/school-specific claim.

## PsyCalc Interpretation

Within PsyCalc, the steering wheel may be useful as a secondary tactical-language map:

- **Good / Bad** can highlight existential identification and rejected information;
- **Need / Should not** can highlight functioning and normative constraints;
- **Problems / Solutions** can highlight self-evaluation and compensatory strategies;
- **Want / Fear** can highlight motivation, suggestibility, and avoidance.

This can enrich qualitative interaction analysis, especially when a user asks about Kalinauskas/Reinin-style Socionics. However, the core Socionics page for PsyCalc remains [[socionics-model-a]].

## Boundaries and Non-Claims

Do not claim that the Kalinauskas steering wheel:

- is universally accepted Socionics core theory;
- replaces Model A;
- is empirically validated;
- proves that opposite types are more complementary than duals in all contexts;
- allows literal “switching into eight types” as an established psychological capacity;
- determines compatibility outcomes by itself.

Use it as an interpretive map with explicit source-status caveats.
