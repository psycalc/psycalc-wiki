# Psychology of Everything Synthesis

## Source status

This page is a **derived synthesis** of an expert-agent discussion on whether people have been interested in a “psychology of everything.” It is not a primary-source literature review and should not be treated as evidence that any one unified psychology framework has scientific consensus.

Evidence label: **derived-synthesis / orientation note**.

## Core finding

Yes: people in academia and online communities have repeatedly searched for something close to a “psychology of everything.” The phrase is not usually standardized. The nearest search clusters are:

- **unified psychology**
- **grand unified theory of psychology**
- **integrative psychology**
- **metatheory of psychology**
- **theoretical psychology**
- **consilience**
- **unified theories of cognition**
- **predictive processing / active inference**
- **global workspace theory**
- **systems psychology**
- **integral theory**

## Major strands

### Unified and integrative psychology

These projects try to integrate fragmented psychological subfields into a broader explanatory framework. Representative names often associated with this cluster include Gregg Henriques, Arthur Staats, Allen Newell, and broader discussions of theoretical psychology and consilience.

### Cognitive architectures

Projects such as ACT-R and SOAR aim to provide unified accounts of cognition, especially memory, attention, learning, and problem solving. They are more constrained than a full “psychology of everything” because they usually focus on cognition rather than all personality, meaning, society, and clinical phenomena.

### Predictive processing and active inference

Predictive processing and active inference frame mind and brain as prediction, error minimization, action, and model updating. This is one of the broadest current neurocognitive meta-frameworks, but it is also criticized for being difficult to operationalize tightly when used too broadly.

### Global workspace theory

Global Workspace Theory and Global Neuronal Workspace models focus on conscious access and the global availability of information. They are not complete personality theories, but they are important integrative frameworks for consciousness and attention.

### Computational cognitive science and network neuroscience

These provide formal and neural-level tools for modeling cognition, control, valuation, attention, and large-scale brain organization. They help build mechanistic bridges, but they do not by themselves produce a full psychology of personality or compatibility.

### Integral and online “maps of everything”

Online communities often gravitate toward large maps that combine psychology, identity, relationships, spirituality, productivity, politics, and meaning. Integral Theory is a prominent example of a popular totalizing framework, though not an academic consensus model.

## Why people look for it

From a social-science perspective, the demand for a “psychology of everything” reflects:

- fragmentation of academic psychology into many subfields;
- information overload and desire for compression;
- online communities built around shared explanatory maps;
- desire for orientation in identity, relationships, work, and meaning;
- frustration with narrow local findings that do not feel like a coherent picture of human life.

## Common risks

- **Overreach:** a framework starts explaining more than it can test.
- **Reification:** metaphors become treated as real entities.
- **Jingle-jangle problems:** same labels for different constructs or different labels for the same construct.
- **Weak measurement:** no reliable operational definitions.
- **Immunity to falsification:** every objection is absorbed into the framework.
- **Social-level collapse:** institutional, class, cultural, or historical causes get reduced to “type” or “mindset.”
- **Clinical/theological overreach:** spiritual, medical, or pathological claims are inferred from personality frameworks.

## Relevance to PsyCalc

PsyCalc is adjacent to this space because it also tries to translate many descriptive systems into a smaller number of latent-process hypotheses. However, PsyCalc should not claim to be a finished “psychology of everything.” A safer positioning is:

> PsyCalc is a research-oriented framework for translating typological systems into testable hypotheses about latent processes relevant to compatibility, role fit, team design, and simulation.

The key distinction is between:

- **orientation map:** useful for organizing ideas;
- **measurement model:** requires construct validity, reliability, invariance, and external criteria;
- **predictive model:** requires outcome data, calibration, baselines, and uncertainty estimates.
