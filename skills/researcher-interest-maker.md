# SKILL: researcher-interest-maker

## Purpose

Make researchers genuinely interested in your project by connecting to their existing work and framing mutual benefit.

## When to Use

- You have a target researcher identified
- You want to start a conversation, not demand a commitment
- You need to cut through inbox noise

## The PACTS Framework

Every outreach message should follow this structure:

```
P - Problem: Name the concrete gap your reader cares about
A - Approach: Say how you solve it
C - Credibility: One crisp proof point
T - Time box: A small, low-risk step
S - Specific ask: A binary yes/no
```

## Step 1: Deep Research

Before writing ANY message:

1. Find 3 recent papers (last 2 years)
2. Read abstracts + conclusions (not full, but substantive)
3. Extract:
   - What problem are they solving?
   - Why does it matter?
   - What's their main contribution?

**Rule**: If you can't summarize their recent work in 2 sentences, you haven't done enough research.

## Step 2: Subject Line

Unskippable format:
- Quote from their paper: `"[Quote from their conclusion]"`
- Or: `[Their topic] × my approach → question`

Generic subject lines ("Research Collaboration") get deleted.

## Step 3: Opening Hook

Start with what YOU learned, not what you want:

```
"I read your recent paper on [topic], especially [specific finding]. 
This connected with [your project angle] because [brief reason]."
```

Shows you've done homework and creates intellectual connection.

## Step 4: Your Credibility

One proof point only:
- A concrete artifact: GitHub link, demo, dataset
- A specific insight: "We found that..."
- Method note: "We've built a prototype that..."

No CV dumps. No lists of qualifications.

## Step 5: The Ask

Smallest possible ask that starts a conversation:

- "Would you have 15 minutes for a quick call?"
- "Could I get your take on [specific question]?"
- "Would you mind if I shared a short evidence packet?"

**Never ask for**:
- Immediate co-authorship
- A large commitment
- A formal collaboration on first contact

## Step 6: Follow-Up

Research shows:
- 1st touch: 20-30% response
- 2nd touch (1 week later): +15%
- 3rd touch (1 week after): +5%

Follow-up formula:
```
"Following up on my earlier note about [topic]. 
Still interested in your take on [one specific question]. 
No pressure if timing isn't right."
```

Each follow-up should shrink the ask.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|--------|-----|
| "Let's collaborate" | Name the one figure or metric |
| Generic subject | Quote from their work |
| Lead with your CV | Lead with their research |
| Big first ask | 15-min call |
| One email only | Follow up 2x |
| Mass personalized | Target 2-3 deeply |

## Message Templates

### First Contact (Collaboration)

```
Subject: [Quote from their conclusion]

Dear Dr. [Name],

I read your recent [paper/post] on [topic], particularly [specific finding]. 
This connects with my work because [brief alignment].

The gap I'm tackling: [one-sentence problem]
My approach: [one-sentence method]
One proof: [one metric or insight]

Would you have 15 minutes for a quick call to explore fit? 
No commitment — just curious if this maps onto anything you're thinking about.

Best,
[Name]
```

### Follow-Up

```
Subject: Following up — [topic]

Hi [Name],

Posted earlier about [topic]. 
Still curious about [one specific question].
If timing isn't right, no worries — but wanted to keep this in your inbox.

Best,
[Name]
```

### Evidence Packet

```
Subject: Evidence packet: [your one-liner]

Hi [Name],

As discussed: [one-liner description]

Evidence packet: [link to doc/sheet]
- The core thesis
- What we've built
- What we're asking for

Happy to hop on a call if useful.

Best,
[Name]
```

## The Mindset

Researchers get boring "collaboration" emails all day. 

What makes you different:
- You did the homework (quote their work)
- You're specific (not vague "collaboration")
- Your ask is small (15 min, not major)
- You respect their time

Your job is to prove you're the kind of person they want to hear from: thoughtful, curious, and collaborative.
